MODULE 04 · MINIMUM NECESSARY STANDARD
Scenario

The Curious Visitor

UNIT: 4 WEST SHIFT: 14:20 ROLE: CHARGE NURSE

A man approaches the nurses' station. He says he's the brother of a patient on your unit and asks what room she's in and how she's doing — he "just flew in and wants to surprise her."

You don't recognize him. The patient hasn't listed visitor restrictions, but she also hasn't confirmed who's allowed to receive updates.

What do you do?
Worth a second look

Family-sounding details aren't verification. Well-intentioned visitors — and people impersonating them — both tend to know just enough to sound convincing.

The safer default is to let the patient decide who hears what, every time, not just when something feels off.

§164.502(b) — Minimum Necessary Standard: disclose only what's needed, only to who's confirmed.
On the mark

Checking with the patient first isn't overcaution — it's the standard. She may want him to know everything, or nothing. That's her call, not an assumption made at the desk.

This single habit prevents the majority of front-desk disclosure incidents.

Knowledge Check

Quick gut-check

A coworker asks you to confirm whether her friend was admitted last night "just to put her mind at ease." What's the right response?

Correct

Being on staff doesn't grant access — being on that patient's care team, or being an authorized contact, does. Curiosity, even kind curiosity, isn't a credential.

Worth a second look

Being a coworker doesn't grant access either — the same standard applies to staff as to anyone else. Being on that patient's care team, or being an authorized contact, is what matters. Curiosity, even kind curiosity, isn't a credential.
